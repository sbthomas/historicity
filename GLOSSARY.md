# Glossary

Technical terms, Greek transliterations, and shorthand references used
in *The Evidentiary Status of the Historical Jesus* and its supplements.
Entries are alphabetised and cross-referenced to the paper section where
each term is developed at length.

---

## Greek terms and transliterations

**Adelphos** (ἀδελφός), plural *adelphoi* (ἀδελφοί). Greek for
"brother." The paper's central lexical argument turns on whether Paul
uses the term in a biological sense or a community-member sense.
*Adelphē* (ἀδελφή) is the feminine "sister." See §3.5 and the
Pauline supplement.

**Adelphos tou Kyriou** (ἀδελφὸν τοῦ κυρίου). "Brother of the Lord."
The phrase in Galatians 1:19 used to identify James, and the single
passage most frequently cited as decisive for the historicity of
Jesus. See §3.5.

**Adelphoi tou Kyriou** (οἱ ἀδελφοὶ τοῦ κυρίου). "The brothers of
the Lord." Plural form in 1 Corinthians 9:5, cited alongside "the
other apostles" and "Cephas" as a recognised class. See §3.5.

**Apokalypsis** (ἀποκάλυψις). "Revelation" or "unveiling." Paul's
explicit term for how his Christ-knowledge was generated (Galatians
1:12, 1:16, 2:2). See §8.2.

**Eispoiētoi adelphoi** (εἰσποιητοὶ ἀδελφοί). "Adopted brothers." A
formulaic self-designation in dedications to Theos Hypsistos at
Tanais on the Sea of Azov. Provides the closest formal parallel in
Greek voluntary-association inscriptions to Paul's *adelphoi tou
kyriou*. See the baseline supplement's epigraphic appendix.

**Iēsous** (Ἰησοῦς). "Jesus"; Greek form of the Hebrew/Aramaic
*Yehoshua* / *Yeshua*, meaning "Yahweh saves." In the mythic-origin
scenario, the name is theologically chosen for its meaning rather
than an inherited proper name of a historical individual. See §11.

**Kata sarka** (κατὰ σάρκα). "According to the flesh." A technical
Pauline phrase used in Romans 1:3 (of Christ's Davidic descent) and
Romans 9:3 (of Paul's ethnic-Jewish kinship). Its precise semantic
reach is contested in scholarship on Pauline Christology. See
§7.3 and §3.5.

**Kyrios** (κύριος). "Lord." In Paul, consistently the title of the
risen, cosmic Christ, not a biographical title for a recently-living
Galilean. Relevant to the interpretation of "brother of *the Lord*"
because the referent governs the semantic range of the genitive.
See §3.5.

**Logos** (λόγος). "Word" or "reason." In Philo of Alexandria, the
divine intermediary figure through whom God creates and acts in the
world; also "God's firstborn son" and "second god" (*deuteros
theos*). A central Jewish-Hellenistic resource for the Christology
Paul develops. See §8.1.

**Merkabah** / **Hekhalot** (מרכבה / היכלות). Jewish mystical
traditions of visionary ascent to the divine chariot-throne
(*merkabah*) of Ezekiel 1, and to the heavenly palaces (*hekhalot*).
The attested procedural framework for how Jewish apocalyptic
communities generated knowledge of heavenly realities. See §8.2.

**Paradidōmi** (παραδίδωμι). "To hand over" or "deliver up." The
verb used in 1 Corinthians 11:23 where Jesus is "handed over"; often
translated "betrayed" to align with the Gospel narrative but whose
primary meaning is theological delivery-as-sacrifice. See §3.2.

**Philadelphia** (φιλαδελφία). "Brotherly love." Abstract kinship
virtue language in Paul, 1 Peter, Hebrews, and elsewhere.

**Pseudadelphos** (ψευδάδελφος). "False brother." Used by Paul at
Galatians 2:4 and 2 Corinthians 11:26. The prefix *pseudo-*
presupposes a community frame for the term "brother."

**Theios anēr** (θεῖος ἀνήρ). "Divine man." A Greco-Roman literary
convention for biographical narratives of figures who combine
divine parentage or empowerment with miracle-working, teaching, and
vindication. Apollonius of Tyana and Pythagoras are canonical
examples. The Gospel Jesus conforms to the type. See §8.4.

**Toledoth Yeshu** (תולדות ישו). Medieval Jewish counter-narrative
compilations about Jesus, likely consolidating earlier oral
polemical traditions. See §6.5.

**Yeshu ha-Notzri** (ישו הנוצרי). "Yeshu the Nazarene." The name
used in several talmudic passages (b.Sanhedrin 43a, 107b; b.Sotah
47a; b.Shabbat 104b). See §6.5.

## Scholarly / historical terms

**Antiquities** (of the Jews). *Antiquitates Judaicae*, the 20-book
history written by Flavius Josephus c. 93–94 CE. Contains the
*Testimonium Flavianum* (AJ 18.63–64) and the James passage (AJ
20.200). See §6.1–§6.2.

**Apostolic Fathers**. A conventional grouping of early Christian
writings from c. 90–150 CE, including 1 Clement, 2 Clement, the
letters of Ignatius, Polycarp to the Philippians, the Epistle of
Barnabas, the Didache, the Martyrdom of Polycarp, the Epistle to
Diognetus, the Shepherd of Hermas, and fragments of Papias.

**Criteria of authenticity**. A set of heuristics developed in
twentieth-century historical-Jesus scholarship to identify sayings
or events likely to derive from a historical Jesus: embarrassment,
multiple attestation, dissimilarity, and coherence. Now widely
recognised as methodologically unreliable. See §2.4.

**Dead Sea Scrolls** / **Qumran**. A body of Jewish manuscripts
discovered 1947–1956, datable from the third century BCE to the
first century CE, representing the library of a sectarian community
(conventionally identified as Essenes) at the Khirbet Qumran site.
Important for the paper because the Scrolls attest pre-Christian
Jewish apocalyptic messianism, angelomorphic mediators, and
apocalyptic-mystical ascent practices. See §8.1–§8.2.

**Deutero-Pauline**. Letters attributed to Paul in the New Testament
but widely regarded as pseudepigraphical: Ephesians, Colossians,
2 Thessalonians, 1 Timothy, 2 Timothy, Titus.

**Dutch Radical school**. A nineteenth-century current of biblical
scholarship (Van Manen, Loman, Pierson) that challenged the
authenticity of the entire Pauline corpus. Largely displaced by the
emerging seven-letter consensus but never systematically refuted;
revived in modern form by Hermann Detering, Markus Vinzent, and
Nina Livesey. See §3.1.

**Euhemerisation**. The historicisation of mythic figures, named
after Euhemerus of Messene (c. 300 BCE), who argued that the gods
had originally been human kings deified after death. The paper
argues that the Paul-to-Mark trajectory is the reverse: a mythic
figure is given a biographical setting. See §9.

**Hellenistic Judaism**. Jewish communities of the post-Alexander
Greek-speaking world, with major centres in Alexandria, Antioch,
and the eastern Mediterranean. Produced the Septuagint, Philo, the
historical-apologetic literature of Josephus, and the context in
which earliest Christianity emerged.

**Markan priority**. The widely-accepted hypothesis that Mark is
the earliest Gospel and that Matthew and Luke used Mark as a source,
either directly (Markan priority without Q, per Goodacre) or with Q
(the two-source hypothesis). See §4.2.

**Marcion-priority**. The hypothesis, advanced in different forms by
Vinzent, Klinghardt, and BeDuhn, that Marcion's mid-second-century
versions of Luke and the Pauline letters are closer to the original
than the canonical versions. See §3.1.

**Memory theory**. A post-criteria framework in historical-Jesus
studies, developed especially by Chris Keith, James Crossley,
Anthony Le Donne, Rafael Rodríguez, and (methodologically) Dale
Allison, that treats the Gospel traditions as shaped by community
memory rather than recoverable via authenticity criteria. See §2.4,
§10.

**NA28**. *Novum Testamentum Graece*, 28th edition
(Nestle–Aland / Deutsche Bibelgesellschaft, 2012). The standard
critical Greek New Testament.

**P46**. The Chester Beatty Biblical Papyrus II (c. 200 CE), our
earliest substantial manuscript of the Pauline corpus. Referenced
throughout the paper's text-critical discussion in §3.1.

**Q hypothesis**. The conjectured "Sayings Source" (from German
*Quelle*, "source") used independently by Matthew and Luke to
supplement their Markan material. Its existence is disputed
(defended by Kloppenborg; denied by Goodacre). See §4.2.

**SBLGNT**. The SBL Greek New Testament, edited by Michael W.
Holmes (2010). A critical edition distributed under Creative
Commons Attribution 4.0, and the textual base used by the Pauline
supplement.

**Second Temple period**. The period of Jewish history from the
rebuilding of the Jerusalem Temple (c. 516 BCE) to its destruction
by the Romans (70 CE). The historical context of Paul, Philo,
Josephus, the Dead Sea Scrolls community, and earliest Christianity.

**Septuagint** (LXX). The Greek translation of the Hebrew Bible
made in Alexandria from the third century BCE onwards. Paul's
scripture.

**Synoptic problem**. The question of the literary relationships
among Matthew, Mark, and Luke, which share substantial verbatim
material suggesting documentary dependence rather than independent
composition. See §4.2 and §4.3.

**Testimonium Flavianum**. The passage in Josephus's *Antiquities*
18.63–64 describing Jesus. Widely regarded as partially or wholly
interpolated. See §6.1.

**Therapeutae**. A Jewish ascetic community near Alexandria
described by Philo (*On the Contemplative Life*). Philo's Therapeutae
passage is the closest parallel in Hellenistic Jewish literature to
the community structure Paul presupposes, and is the single clear
Category-A (voluntary-association *adelphoi*) use in the Philonic
corpus. See the baseline supplement.

## Classification scheme (A/B/C/D/E)

Used throughout the Pauline supplement and the baseline supplement
to classify occurrences of the *adelph-* family of lexemes.

**A** — **Community / voluntary association**. Member of a
religious, philosophical, professional, or cultic group joined by
election. Christian "brothers in Christ"; guild members; cult
associates; Stoic school members; *pseudadelphoi* (false brothers,
where the "false" prefix presupposes a community frame).

**B** — **Unambiguously biological sibling**. Shares at least one
biological parent with the referent. Blood relationship through
descent.

**C** — **Disputed or ambiguous**. Context permits both a kinship
and a community reading, or neither cleanly. Gal 1:19 and
1 Cor 9:5 "of the Lord" tokens fall here; borderline cases default
to C rather than the coder's best guess.

**D** — **Ethnic / tribal / covenantal kinship**. Shared identity
larger than family but acquired by birth: fellow-Israelite,
fellow-tribe-member, fellow-Greek as ethnos. Romans 9:3 "my
brothers, my kinsmen *kata sarka*" is the canonical Pauline example.

**E** — **Literary / metaphorical extension**. Poetic, rhetorical,
or philosophical figure: "sister-virtues" in Philo; "brothers of
Zeus" in Epictetus's cosmopolitan arguments; "fire as brother to
the ether" in Plutarch's metaphysical prose.

## Citation shorthand

**AJ** — *Antiquitates Judaicae* (Josephus's *Antiquities*).
**BJ** — *Bellum Judaicum* (Josephus's *Jewish War*).
**b.Sanh.** — Babylonian Talmud, tractate Sanhedrin.
**CIL** — *Corpus Inscriptionum Latinarum* (standard Latin inscription
corpus).
**GRA** — Kloppenborg & Ascough, *Greco-Roman Associations: Texts,
Translations, and Commentary* (the standard sourcebook for
association inscriptions).
**IBosp** — *Inscriptiones Bosporani*.
**IG** — *Inscriptiones Graecae*.
**IPrusaOlymp** — Inscriptions of Prusa by Olympos.
**PHI** — Packard Humanities Institute (Greek inscriptions database).
**SBL** — Society of Biblical Literature.
**SEG** — *Supplementum Epigraphicum Graecum*.
**TLG** — *Thesaurus Linguae Graecae*.
