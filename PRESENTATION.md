# The Question

## Does the evidence warrant the confidence of the consensus?

- Ehrman 2012: "virtually every competent scholar\ldots agrees" Jesus existed
- Dunn 2003: "today nearly all historians\ldots accept that Jesus existed"
- These are consensus claims, not evidential arguments.
- Consensus about ancient persons is not the same thing as primary attestation.

## Claim of this paper

The evidence does *not* establish historicity with the confidence the
consensus claims. The mythic-origin hypothesis, in its
Jewish-apocalyptic (not pagan-mystery-cult) form, is a serious
alternative that survives scrutiny of its own weaknesses.

\medskip

**Not**: Jesus definitely did not exist.
**Not**: Mythicism is proven.
**Yes**: The question is more open than the consensus admits.

# Method

## Four methodological commitments

1. **Consensus is not evidence.** Useful heuristic when evidential
   judgements are independent; less so when institutional formation
   presupposes the conclusion.
2. **Bayesian reasoning with source-dependence.** Tucker 2004: cumulative
   cases require independence. Source-dependent testimonies (Gospels →
   Mark, Tacitus → Christian sources) must be discounted.
3. **Absence of evidence = evidence of absence** when evidence would be
   expected under the hypothesis.
4. **Criteria of authenticity have collapsed** (Keith 2024, Allison
   2010). Gospels cannot serve as positive evidence for a historical core.

## Comparative perspective

Other contested existence-questions and what settled them:

- **Pilate**: confirmed by 1961 Caesarea inscription.
- **Socrates**: multiple independent contemporary attestation (Plato, Xenophon, Aristophanes, Aristotle).
- **Moses**: rejected by Egyptian archaeological silence.
- **Lycurgus, Romulus**: rejected on literary-critical grounds.
- **Pythagoras, Arthur, Homer, Laozi**: genuinely contested.

Jesus's evidentiary profile most closely matches the contested category.

# The Pauline Evidence

## Paul's Jesus

- No parents, birthplace, hometown named.
- No ministry, miracles, teachings, parables.
- No Pilate, Judas, Golgotha, Jerusalem.
- Explicitly: knowledge of Christ came by *apokalypsis* (Gal 1:12--17)
  and scripture (1 Cor 15:3--4).

This is the epistemic profile of a Jewish mystical visionary
(Scholem, Morton Smith, Rowland, Segal, Gruenwald), not of a disciple
of a recently-deceased teacher.

## The *adelphos tou kyriou* passages (Gal 1:19, 1 Cor 9:5)

The supposed "smoking gun" for historicity. Three complications:

1. Paul uses *adelphos* 117 times in community-sense and **zero** times
   in unambiguously biological sense outside the two contested passages.
2. 1 Cor 9:5 uses the *plural* "brothers of the Lord" as a class label
   parallel to "apostles" and "Cephas" — a group designation, not a
   biological claim.
3. Greek religious-association inscriptions (Harland 2005,
   Kloppenborg-Ascough 2011) attest *adelphoi tou* [patron figure] as a
   formally-normal construction for community members.

## Corpus-linguistic baseline (supplementary study)

212 random-sampled *adelphos* tokens across six non-Christian Greek
corpora:

| Corpus | A% (community) | B% (biological) |
|---|---|---|
| Papyri pre-70 CE | 0 | 100 |
| LXX | 0 | 69 |
| Philo | 3 | 57 |
| Pre-Pauline literary | 3 | 94 |
| **Aggregate** | **0.9%** | **77%** |
| **Paul** | **96.7%** | **0%** |

Paul's usage is distinctive against everyday Greek; *not* distinctive
against Greek religious-associative register.

## Conceded difficulties

- **Rom 1:3--4** "descended from David *kata sarka*": pre-Pauline
  creed, textually secure in P46. Novenson's Davidic-messianism point
  applies. Mythic-origin responses exist (pre-Pauline formula, *kata
  sarka* as sublunar-realm phrase) but do not fully dissolve the
  tension.
- **Gal 4:4** "born of a woman, born under the law": mild favouring of
  historicist reading.
- **1 Cor 15:3--8 witness list**: "over 500 brothers" is the hardest
  Pauline datum for mythic-origin. Mythic-origin response: multiple
  community members' visionary experience of a heavenly figure is
  attested in Jewish mystical-apocalyptic contexts (Qumran Self-
  Glorification Hymn, 1 Enoch).

These are real tensions, honestly acknowledged.

## Textual and stylometric stability

- All contested historicity-relevant passages are attested in P46
  (c. 200 CE) and in Marcion's reconstructed text.
- Preliminary stylometric analysis (*scripts/stylometry.py*) validates
  on known interpolations (1 Thess 2:14--16, 1 Cor 14:34--35: outliers
  correctly flagged) and does *not* flag Gal 1:19 or Rom 1:3 as
  interpolation candidates.
- Mythic-origin arguments must therefore proceed on semantic and
  contextual grounds, not on text-critical excision.

# The Jewish-Apocalyptic Matrix

## Not pagan mystery cults

Older mythicism (Frazer 1890) located sources in pagan dying-and-rising
gods (Osiris, Attis, Adonis). **This is the weaker version of the
argument and not the one defended here.**

Fredriksen 2017 is correct: Paul is a Second Temple Jewish
apocalyptic thinker operating within Jewish categories.

## The Jewish resources that suffice

- **Pre-existent heavenly mediators**: Danielic Son of Man
  (Dan 7:13-14), Similitudes of Enoch (1 Enoch 37-71), Melchizedek
  at Qumran (11QMelch), Philo's Logos.
- **Vicarious suffering**: Isaiah 52-53 Suffering Servant; Wisdom of
  Solomon 2-5 righteous-one-persecuted-and-vindicated pattern.
- **Revelatory mechanism**: Merkabah/Hekhalot visionary ascent; Qumran
  Self-Glorification Hymn; Ascension of Isaiah; Paul's 2 Cor 12:2-4
  "caught up to the third heaven."
- **Crucifixion specifically**: Deut 21:23 "cursed is everyone who
  hangs on a tree," cited by Paul in Gal 3:13 as the engine of
  substitutionary-curse soteriology.

Every element of Paul's Christology is derivable from pre-Christian
Jewish sources. No pagan borrowing required.

# The Other Evidence

## The Gospels

Built from identifiable Hebrew Bible templates:

- Passion: Psalm 22, Isaiah 53, Wisdom 2-5, Zechariah, Daniel 7.
- Birth: Moses/Samuel/Isaac typology.
- Ministry: Elijah-Elisha cycle.

**This establishes that the Gospels are not independent historical
testimony, not that no historical subject underlies them.** The
evidential burden shifts entirely to non-Gospel strata.

## Non-Christian attestation

- **Josephus *Testimonium***: partially or wholly interpolated
  (Olson 1999, Carrier 2014).
- **Josephus James passage**: accepted by most scholars; attests the
  Christian claim as of 93-94 CE, not independently to a first-century
  figure.
- **Tacitus *Annals* 15.44**: dependent on Christian informants in Rome.
- **Pliny, Suetonius, rabbinic, Mandaean**: too late or too dependent
  on Christian tradition.

Net: confirms early circulation of a historicized narrative. Does not
independently confirm the narrative.

## Silence

Expected attestation missing across multiple independent channels:

- Philo of Alexandria (contemporary, wrote on Pilate, messianic
  expectation, Logos).
- Justus of Tiberias (native Galilean historian; Photius explicitly
  notes the silence).
- Mishnaic tradition (weaker, genre limitations).
- Roman administrative records (moderated by general loss).

Cumulative weight is real but modest, not decisive.

# The Synthesis

## Comparative evidentiary profile

Jesus's profile most closely matches the **contested** category:

- **Unlike Pilate**: no direct epigraphic attestation.
- **Unlike Socrates**: no multiple independent contemporary literary
  attestation (Paul is one step removed; Gospels post-date by 40+ years
  and are non-independent).
- **Like Pythagoras/Arthur/Homer/Laozi**: probable historical kernel
  with massive legendary elaboration *or* community construction to
  satisfy founder-myth requirements, with the evidence insufficient to
  discriminate.

## Bayesian sensitivity analysis

Under plausible point-estimate likelihood ratios (see
*scripts/bayesian_sensitivity.py*) and a neutral prior $P(H) = 0.5$:

- Independence-assumed posterior: **0.39**
- Dependency-aware posterior: **0.23**

Both in open-question territory.

Under strong historicist prior $P(H) = 0.9$:

- Independence-assumed: 0.85
- Dependency-aware: 0.73

Still materially below the near-certainty the consensus projects.

# Conclusion

## What the evidence supports

- The Pauline corpus describes a revelatory celestial figure assembled
  from pre-Christian Jewish apocalyptic-mystical sources.
- The Gospels are *not* independent historical testimony.
- The non-Christian attestation confirms early circulation of the
  Christian claim, not the claim itself.
- The *adelphos tou kyriou* passages admit a community-title reading
  that instantiates a well-attested Greek religious-associative
  convention.
- The question is genuinely open.

## What the evidence does not support

- Near-certainty of historicity.
- Dismissal of mythic-origin hypotheses as fringe.
- The consensus as independent evidence.
