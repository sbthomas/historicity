# Two Thousand Years Later, a New Question About the Most Famous Man in History

*The consensus says he existed. A new paper says the evidence is thinner than that consensus lets on.*

**By a General-Audience Rewrite** — Draft, April 2026

---

Stan Thomas is not a theologian. He does not teach at a divinity
school. He does not have a book deal. He lives in St. George, Utah,
and in his spare time, over the past year, he has written an
85-page paper arguing that the case for the historical Jesus — the
Galilean preacher crucified under Pontius Pilate around the year 30 —
is considerably weaker than most people, including most scholars,
assume.

This is the kind of project that makes serious historians groan
before they finish the abstract. The field has seen this movie
before. In the late nineteenth century, a German scholar named
Bruno Bauer argued Jesus was a literary invention; the Dutch Radical
school made similar claims a generation later; and more recently,
figures like Richard Carrier and Raphael Lataster have made the
case in forms that most biblical scholars consider an embarrassment
to engage with. Bart Ehrman, the University of North Carolina
scholar who is the most prominent secular voice defending the
historicity of Jesus, once observed that "virtually every competent
scholar of antiquity, Christian or non-Christian, agrees" that
Jesus existed.

So it is fair to ask why anyone should care about a new contribution
to what looks like a settled question. The short answer is that
Thomas's paper does not actually try to prove that Jesus did not
exist. It tries to prove something narrower, and in some ways more
disturbing to the scholarly field: that the confidence with which
most experts assert his existence is not justified by the evidence
they have.

That claim, if taken seriously, changes what biblical scholarship is
doing.

---

### What we actually have

Start with Paul. The letters of the Apostle Paul to the churches in
Corinth, Rome, Galatia and elsewhere are the earliest Christian
documents that survive. They were written, scholars agree, in the
50s CE — within a generation of the supposed crucifixion, and
fifteen to twenty years before any of the Gospels were composed.

If you sat down expecting Paul to tell you about Jesus — the man,
the teacher, the movement leader — you would be surprised. Paul
never mentions the place of Jesus's birth. He never mentions his
mother or father by name. He does not describe a single parable,
miracle, or public confrontation. He does not say where the
crucifixion happened. He does not identify Pilate. He does not
identify Judas as a betrayer. He does not mention Mary Magdalene,
or Peter's denial, or the Last Supper (except as a liturgical
event received "from the Lord"), or any of the narrative material
that fills the Gospels.

What Paul describes, over and over, is a cosmic figure known to him
and his communities through revelation (*apokalypsis*, in Greek) and
through the Hebrew scriptures read as encoded prophecy. "I did not
receive it from a human source," Paul writes in Galatians, "nor was
I taught it, but I received it through a revelation of Jesus
Christ."

The single clearest biographical-sounding phrase in Paul's entire
corpus is four words in his letter to the Galatians: *adelphos tou
kyriou*, "brother of the Lord," used of a man named James in
Jerusalem whom Paul says he met. That phrase has carried enormous
weight in modern scholarship. Ehrman has called it, essentially by
itself, decisive evidence for the historicity of Jesus: if Jesus had
a biological brother whom Paul met, Jesus existed.

Thomas's paper, which is available as a preprint at
github.com/sbthomas/historicity, agrees that the phrase is real.
But it argues that "brother" in Paul's Greek overwhelmingly does
not mean biological sibling. Of the 121 times Paul uses the word
*adelphos* and its variants, 117 refer to fellow members of the
early Christian community — the "brothers in Christ" usage any
reader of Paul's letters will recognize. Zero of the remaining
occurrences unambiguously denote a biological brother of anyone.
Which raises a question: if "brother" in Paul's corpus is
overwhelmingly a community term, why is the single phrase
"brother of the Lord" being translated as a kinship claim rather
than as a community title?

---

### A number nobody had calculated

This is where Thomas's paper does something that has not, to his
knowledge, been done before. He compiles a statistical sample of
how the word *adelphos* is actually used in Greek-language texts
from the two centuries surrounding Paul's lifetime — ancient
letters scrawled on papyrus, the Greek translation of the Hebrew
Bible, the writings of Philo of Alexandria, the works of Josephus,
Plutarch, Epictetus, Polybius, Strabo, Diodorus. He draws 405
random tokens, classifies each one, and computes the base rate for
a "community of fellow members" usage.

The rate is 0.7 percent. Paul's rate is 96.7 percent.

This extreme difference is not, as such, a proof of anything. Thomas
is careful to note that his sample excludes a relevant register:
Greek inscriptions from religious associations across the eastern
Mediterranean, where the word *adelphoi* was the normal term for
fellow members of cult societies. A scholar named Philip Harland
documented these inscriptions in a 2005 article. Among them are
nine dedications to a deity called Theos Hypsistos at a Greek-Iranian
frontier town called Tanais, where members of the association
called themselves, formulaically, "adopted brothers who revere
Theos Hypsistos" — structurally identical to Paul's "brothers of
the Lord."

Factoring the inscriptional evidence in, the gap between Paul and
the ambient baseline shrinks. But the interpretive consequence runs
the opposite direction from what a defender of Jesus's historicity
would like. Far from isolating Paul as an idiosyncratic stylist,
the inscriptions show that Paul was writing in a well-attested
Greek register in which *adelphoi* of a patron figure was a normal
community designation. The more natural reading of "brother of the
Lord," when placed in its actual Greek religious-associative
context, is that it names James as a member of a group within the
Pauline movement, not as the biological sibling of a historical
Galilean.

### What the Gospels can and cannot show

The Gospels — Mark, Matthew, Luke, John — present a different
problem. They were written between 40 and 70 years after the
supposed events, by authors whose identities we do not know, in
Greek, for audiences that were not in Palestine. None of them claims
to be an eyewitness account.

Biblical scholarship has for more than a century recognized that the
narrative details of the Gospels are, to a strong degree, constructed
from Hebrew Bible templates. The passion narrative of Mark maps,
verse by verse, onto Psalm 22 ("My God, my God, why have you
forsaken me?"), Isaiah 53 ("he was numbered with the transgressors"),
the Wisdom of Solomon 2–5, and Zechariah. The ministry reprises
the Elijah-Elisha miracle cycle. The birth narratives are
transparently built from proof-texts, and Matthew's and Luke's
birth narratives contradict each other so sharply that they cannot
both be historical.

Thomas's paper grants a point here that some mythicist writing has
missed. "Demonstrating that a narrative is constructed from
scriptural templates," he writes, "does not on its own prove that
no subject underlies it." Historical figures have been described
through scriptural grammars in antiquity — the biographies of the
Maccabean heroes are a case in point. What the Gospel material
shows is not the non-existence of a historical Jesus, but rather
that the Gospels cannot be used as independent testimony for his
existence. The question of historicity then has to be decided on
other evidence.

That other evidence turns out to be thin. Josephus, writing in the
90s CE, has two passages mentioning Jesus. The longer one, the
*Testimonium Flavianum*, is almost universally agreed to have been
tampered with by later Christian copyists; how much of it is
original is contested. The shorter James passage is accepted as
largely genuine, but the phrase Josephus uses — "brother of Jesus
called Christ" — may itself be a later insertion, and in any case
carries limited evidential weight almost a century after the
supposed events, from an author writing in Rome for a Roman
audience who probably learned what he knew from the Christian
communities he encountered.

Tacitus, writing around 116 CE, mentions Christ's execution by
Pilate, but almost certainly draws his information from Christian
informants rather than from Roman imperial records. The rabbinic
traditions about Yeshu, including the later *Toledot Yeshu*
compilations, are polemical engagements with the Christian
narrative, not independent attestations of an earlier figure.

What would settle the question is the kind of evidence that settled
the historicity of Pilate himself: a first-century inscription, or
a contemporary document, or an artifact contemporary with Jesus's
life that names him. No such object has ever been found. The 2002
James Ossuary, which briefly looked like it might be such an
artifact, was found by an Israeli antiquities investigation to bear
a modern forgery.

### The honest concessions

What makes Thomas's paper unusual in the mythicist genre is its
willingness to acknowledge the places where the mythic-origin
hypothesis is difficult to defend.

Three Pauline passages in particular are, in his assessment,
"genuine challenges." Romans 1:3 describes Christ as "descended
from David according to the flesh" — a specifically genealogical,
specifically historical, specifically Jewish-messianic claim.
Galatians 4:4 says Christ was "born of a woman, born under the
law." And the witness list in 1 Corinthians 15 names specific
people — Cephas, James, the Twelve, five hundred brothers at once,
Paul himself — to whom the risen Christ is said to have appeared.

These are not easy passages for a purely mythic reading. Thomas's
paper engages them, offers possible readings (they are pre-Pauline
creedal formulas; "according to the flesh" can mark a descent into
the sublunar realm of flesh rather than a biographical lineage;
the witness list reads less like a biographical record than like a
list of visionary recipients), but does not claim to have fully
resolved them. The concluding chapter is explicit: "Several features
of the evidence pose genuine difficulties for the mythicist model
that have not been fully resolved."

The paper also offers a constructive hypothesis, not just a
destructive critique. Drawing on the 1941 work of Gershom Scholem
on Jewish mysticism and the more recent work of Alan Segal on Paul
as a visionary-mystic, it argues that the earliest Christian
community looks exactly like a Second Temple Jewish apocalyptic-
mystical sect that generated its knowledge of a heavenly mediator
figure through the well-attested procedures of visionary ascent
and scriptural exegesis — the same procedures that produced the
*Similitudes of Enoch*, the *Apocalypse of Abraham*, the *Ascension
of Isaiah*, and the Dead Sea Scrolls' Self-Glorification Hymn. The
Christ Paul knew, on this reading, is a named heavenly figure
known to the community by revelation. The historicization — the
setting of that figure in recent Galilean time and place — would
come later, with the Gospel of Mark around 70 CE.

This is much closer to the scholarly mainstream than most mythicist
writing gets. It does not invoke pagan dying-and-rising mystery
cults. It does not claim Paul was a Gnostic. It does not deny that
Christianity is rooted in Jewish thought. It simply argues that the
specific Jewish thought-world it is rooted in — visionary-ascent
apocalypticism — does not require a historical founder to explain
what the early letters say.

### What the field has not been asking

Thomas is not the first person to make parts of this argument.
What is unusual is how his paper handles its own weaknesses. It
concedes explicitly. It lists by name the scholars whose work
most threatens its conclusions — Dale Allison of Princeton
Theological Seminary, Paula Fredriksen of Boston University,
Matthew Novenson of Edinburgh, Harland at York University — and
walks through what each of them would likely say in response. It
includes, as companion documents, a comprehensive bibliography, a
list of explicit falsification conditions, and a document titled
*Responses* that anticipates the reviewer objections it will draw.

"The question is not whether scholars agree," the paper's
methodological preface says, "but whether the evidence warrants
their agreement."

That framing may be the most provocative thing in the document.
The scholarly study of the historical Jesus emerged, historically,
from within divinity schools and confessional faculties. Even the
field's most rigorous critics — Bart Ehrman, who is an agnostic;
Gerd Theissen, a sociologist; John Dominic Crossan, a former priest
— came up through institutions in which the existence of Jesus was
a shared premise of the enterprise. The field has been intensely
rigorous about almost every other aspect of the tradition: about
which sayings are authentic, about the Synoptic problem, about
textual transmission, about the development of doctrine. What it
has not, as a field, been in the habit of asking is whether its
shared starting premise holds.

Whether the premise does hold is not a question that a single paper
by an independent researcher from Utah can settle. But the paper
is not asking to settle it. It is asking for the premise to be
examined with the same tools the field uses to examine everything
else.

---

### The stakes

None of this undermines the cultural, theological, or ethical
significance of the Christian tradition. A figure assembled from
Jewish scripture and Hellenistic religious imagination is not less
philosophically consequential than a figure assembled from oral
memory and community tradition. The Sermon on the Mount is what
it is regardless of whether it was spoken on a hillside or
composed in a community in Antioch.

What is at stake is not theology. It is what the discipline of
history looks like when it applies its own evidentiary standards
consistently.

There is, on the last page of Thomas's paper, a line that reads
less like an argument and more like a challenge: "If the field
cannot bring itself to treat the question as open — if the mere
posing of the question continues to be treated as a mark of
incompetence rather than as a legitimate scholarly inquiry — then
the field has a problem that no amount of consensus can resolve."

Ehrman, for what it is worth, is unlikely to change his mind. Dale
Allison is more likely to engage. Paula Fredriksen will probably
not be persuaded but may push the argument in productive
directions. Philip Harland, the specialist on *adelphoi* in Greek
religious associations, is the scholar whose work is most directly
cited, and whose reaction will be most informative, if he chooses
to respond.

Whether any of them does is the real test. For two thousand years,
the question of whether Jesus of Nazareth existed has been answered
with a confidence that the evidence itself does not quite support.
A researcher in St. George, Utah, has written an 85-page paper
asking the field to notice.
