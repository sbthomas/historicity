# Anticipated Reviewer Critiques and Responses

A companion document to *The Evidentiary Status of the Historical
Jesus: A Critical Assessment* and its supplements. This document
preempts the specific objections most likely to be raised by peer
reviewers from different parts of the scholarly landscape, with
cross-references to the paper where the objection is addressed and
an explicit accounting of which objections the paper concedes.

---

## A. Critiques from mainstream historicists

### A1. Ehrman-style: "This is fringe; competent scholars don't dispute historicity"

**Objection.** *The consensus of competent New Testament scholars
is that Jesus existed; mythicism is a fringe position entertained
only by non-specialists.*

**Response.** The paper addresses this in §2.1 and §10. Consensus
is a heuristic, not evidence. Its epistemic weight depends on
whether the underlying judgements are independent. Since the
historical-Jesus field has been conducted primarily within
institutions that presuppose historicity (§10), the consensus
operates as the starting premise rather than the conclusion. The
paper does not dismiss the consensus; it treats it as a datum to
be explained alongside the evidence. Recent credentialed
scholarship sympathetic to or developing mythic-origin scenarios
(Carrier 2014, Lataster 2019, Brodie 2012, Price 2012) and recent
academic Paul-skeptical work (Vinzent 2023, Livesey 2024 [CUP],
Klinghardt 2015, BeDuhn 2013) is not fringe; it is active
scholarship that must be engaged rather than dismissed.

### A2. Fredriksen-style: "Paul is Jewish apocalyptic, not pagan-mystery"

**Objection.** *The paper imposes a Hellenistic pagan-mystery-cult
framework on a thinker who is plainly operating within Second
Temple Jewish apocalyptic categories. Paul's use of Rom 1:3
(Davidic descent) and Gal 4:4 (born of a woman, born under the
law) are statements of radical Jewish embodiment, not a celestial
pagan import.*

**Response.** This is the strongest single thesis-level challenge,
and the paper has been substantially restructured to accommodate
it. §8 has been rewritten from a "mythological parallels" section
grounded in Frazerian pagan cults to "Prior Sources: The Jewish-
Apocalyptic Matrix" grounded explicitly in Second Temple Jewish
resources (Danielic Son of Man, Philo's Logos, angelomorphic
mediators, 1 Enoch's Similitudes, the Qumran Self-Glorification
Hymn). The claim the paper now defends is that every major
element of Paul's Christology is derivable from pre-Christian
Jewish apocalyptic-mystical material *without recourse to pagan
dying-and-rising cults*. This is the same position Carrier 2014
explicitly develops. Fredriksen (2017, 2018) is correct that Paul
is a Jewish apocalyptic thinker; the paper now argues, on her
premises, that a Jewish apocalyptic origin of the Christ figure
does not require a historical subject any more than 1 Enoch's Son
of Man required a historical subject. See especially §8.2 on the
*apokalypsis* mechanism, which grounds the Jewish-mystical
procedural framework in Scholem, Rowland, Segal, Gruenwald, and
Schäfer.

**Conceded points.** Rom 1:3 and Gal 4:4 remain genuine
difficulties for mythic-origin scenarios. The paper explicitly
acknowledges this in §3.2, §8.3, and the conclusion. The
mythic-origin response to these passages is that they are pre-
Pauline creedal fragments that need not express Paul's own
primary categories, and that *kata sarka* can refer to the
sublunar realm of flesh in a celestial-incarnation framework.
Neither response fully dissolves the tension. The paper does not
pretend to have resolved this.

### A3. Novenson-style: "Davidic messianism is genuinely human-historical in Second Temple Judaism"

**Objection.** *Novenson's work on the grammar of messianism shows
that messianic language in Second Temple Judaism has specific
constraints: a messiah is a human figure of a specific royal
lineage. Paul's Rom 1:3 "descended from David according to the
flesh" anchors the Christ figure in this human-genealogical
grammar.*

**Response.** Engaged directly in §8.3. This is a subset of the
Fredriksen objection and the strongest specific version. The
paper acknowledges the difficulty and offers three possible
responses (pre-Pauline creedal fragment, *kata sarka* as sublunar-
realm technical phrase, scriptural-exegesis origin of the Davidic
claim), none of which fully dissolves the tension. Rom 1:3
remains one of the strongest historicist data points. The paper's
overall argument does not depend on dissolving it; it depends on
the claim that the cumulative case is weaker than the consensus
holds, which survives this concession.

### A4. Allison-style: "Templating is not identical to non-historicity"

**Objection.** *The paper shows that the Gospels are constructed
from Hebrew Bible templates. But historical figures can be
described through scriptural grammars; Jewish historiography
routinely templates the lives of real people through HB typology.
Templating establishes compositional method, not non-historicity.*

**Response.** Explicitly acknowledged in §4 (Gospel Evidence).
The logical step the argument makes is narrower and clearer than
earlier mythicist writing has sometimes implied. Templating
establishes that *the Gospels are not independent historical
testimony*, not that *no historical subject underlies the
narrative*. The historicist evidential burden therefore shifts
entirely to non-Gospel strata: Paul and the non-Christian
attestation. §3 argues Paul is insufficient for the burden; §6
argues the non-Christian attestation is insufficient. The
cumulative case breaks at the joints, not at the Gospels
specifically. Allison 2010 and 2021 are engaged in §10
(Sociology / Engagement with Anti-Mythicist Scholarship) and
explicitly named as the strongest sustained historicist case in
print.

### A5. Ehrman-style: "The Peter-James-Paul nexus"

**Objection.** *Paul met Peter and James in Jerusalem (Gal 1-2);
both are near-certain historical persons; Paul refers to James
specifically as "the brother of the Lord" (Gal 1:19). Mythic-
origin scenarios have to explain this chain of acquaintance.*

**Response.** Engaged in §3.5. The Peter-James-Paul meeting is
uncontested; Paul says he met them. What is contested is the
interpretation of the *adelphos tou kyriou* phrase: under the
community-title reading, James is a prominent member of a
recognised group called "Brothers of the Lord" (as 1 Cor 9:5
attests), which does not entail biological kinship with Jesus.
Under the mythic-origin scenario, Peter and James are historical
early leaders of a religious-associative community centred on a
revealed celestial Christ; their historicity does not require
the historicity of the figure their community reveres.
Josephus AJ 20.200 is discussed in §6.2 and not dismissed; its
evidential weight is accepted as real but limited (it attests
the Christian claim as of c. 93-94 CE, not independently to a
first-century individual).

### A6. Keith/Crossley-style: "Memory theory replaces criteria"

**Objection.** *Recent scholarship has moved past the criteria of
authenticity to memory theory. Memory studies show how communal
memory preserves historical kernels even when narrative detail
is elaborated. The gestalt of the tradition is better explained
by memory of a historical figure than by literary construction.*

**Response.** Engaged in §2.4 (criteria) and §10 (anti-mythicist
scholarship). The paper accepts that criteria of authenticity are
collapsed and that memory theory is the current framework. It
argues that memory-theory arguments about gestalt preservation
require an assumption that memory rather than exegesis is the
primary generative source of the gestalt — an assumption that
requires independent defence. §8 argues that Jewish scriptural
and apocalyptic-mystical generative procedures (exegesis plus
revelatory experience) can produce the same gestalt features
without memory of a historical figure. Memory theory does not on
its own discriminate between the hypotheses.

## B. Critiques from mythicist-sympathetic readers

### B1. Carrier-style: "Concede less; multiply likelihood ratios"

**Objection.** *The paper concedes too much on Rom 1:3, Gal 4:4,
1 Cor 15, and Josephus 20.200. A proper Bayesian multiplication
of likelihood ratios shows the posterior against historicity is
much lower than the paper suggests.*

**Response.** The paper adopts explicit dependency-aware Bayesian
reasoning in §2.2 and *scripts/bayesian_sensitivity.py*. The
multiplication of likelihood ratios assuming independence (which
Carrier's approach implicitly does) overcounts when sources are
textually or socially dependent (Tacitus→Christian informants,
Gospels→Mark). Accounting for dependency compresses posteriors
toward the prior. The paper's concessions on specific passages
are not tactical but substantive: Rom 1:3 is in P46, is textually
secure, and is a pre-Pauline creed — these facts do not favour
mythicism, and pretending otherwise would weaken the paper's
credibility. The paper's position is that responsible mythic-
origin scenarios must acknowledge these difficulties rather than
dismiss them.

### B2. "Why not stronger mythicism?"

**Objection.** *The paper's Bayesian analysis lands on posterior
P(H) in the 0.23-0.39 range. That's still not a strong mythic-
origin case. Why not argue for P(H) < 0.1?*

**Response.** The paper's claim is calibrated to the evidence as
the author understands it. A lower posterior would require either
(a) stronger point estimates of likelihood ratios for the silence
arguments, or (b) dismissal of the conceded-difficulty Pauline
passages (Rom 1:3 etc.), or (c) acceptance of full-interpolation
theses for the Josephan James passage. The paper does not rule
out a lower posterior — it explicitly documents the sensitivity
of the result to these point estimates in
*scripts/bayesian_sensitivity.py* — but does not claim one.

### B3. "Ignores Marcion-priority scholarship"

**Objection.** *Vinzent, Klinghardt, and BeDuhn have substantially
revised the Pauline corpus's textual-critical baseline by arguing
for Marcion-priority. The paper does not sufficiently engage
this.*

**Response.** Engaged explicitly in §3.1 ("Current Paul-skeptical
scholarship" paragraph). The paper cites Vinzent 2014 and 2023,
Klinghardt 2015, BeDuhn 2013, and Livesey 2024 and notes that
these positions are credentialed, published by reputable academic
presses, and cannot be dismissed. The paper *does not adopt* the
Marcion-priority hypothesis as a load-bearing premise because it
wants its argument to hold under the conservative seven-letter
authenticity consensus. If Marcion-priority is correct, the
argument is strengthened (some historicizing phrases become
interpolation-suspicious); if it is not, the argument stands on
its own grounds.

## C. Methodological critiques

### C1. "Single-coder A/B/C/D/E classification"

**Objection.** *All 212 baseline classifications and all 121
Pauline classifications were made by the paper's author. No
inter-rater reliability is assessed. This is a substantial
methodological limitation that invalidates quantitative claims.*

**Response.** Explicitly flagged as the principal limitation in
*historicity-baseline.pdf* ("Limitations" section). Future work
includes securing an external coder (Greek-literate, neutral on
historicity) to independently classify a subset and compute
Cohen's κ. This is listed as TODO item #5 at
<https://github.com/sbthomas/historicity/blob/main/TODO.md>. The
paper's substantive claims survive a realistic range of inter-
coder disagreement — the qualitative finding that Paul's usage is
distinctive against everyday Greek but fits Greek associative
register is robust to reasonable variation in individual
classifications.

### C2. "Inscriptions excluded from baseline"

**Objection.** *The baseline supplement excludes Greek
inscriptions, the very register where community-sense adelphoi
concentrates. This inflates the 103x Paul-to-baseline ratio. Is
the paper's quantitative claim actually supported?*

**Response.** Fully acknowledged and corrected. The 103x figure
is explicitly *disavowed* in *historicity-baseline.pdf* and in
the main paper's §3.5. The corrected estimate incorporating
inscriptional evidence (Harland 2005, Kloppenborg-Ascough 2011)
is approximately 10-20x. Twenty-five inscriptional cases of
community-sense adelphoi are documented in the Epigraphic
Appendix of *historicity-baseline.pdf*. The qualitative finding
(Paul's usage is distinctive against everyday Greek but fits the
Greek religious-associative sub-register) survives the
correction.

### C3. "Stylometry uses arbitrary feature list"

**Objection.** *Stylometric analysis depends critically on
feature-list choice. Different function-word inventories would
yield different outlier patterns.*

**Response.** Acknowledged in *scripts/stylometry.py* and §3.1.
The analysis is presented as exploratory first-pass, not
definitive stylometric authorship-attribution. The feature list
(~80 high-frequency function words) is validated by correctly
flagging known interpolations (1 Thess 2:14-16, 1 Cor 14:34-35)
as outliers and known pre-Pauline creeds (Rom 1:3-4, 1 Cor 15:3-5,
Phil 2:5-11) as stylometrically distinct. The negative result for
historicity-relevant passages (Gal 1:19 in context not an outlier)
is *more* robust than the positive findings because a null
result under a method that validates on controls is epistemically
stronger than a positive result under an unvalidated method.

### C4. "Bayesian sensitivity point estimates are subjective"

**Objection.** *The likelihood ratios in
scripts/bayesian_sensitivity.py are point estimates that are
contestable.*

**Response.** Explicitly acknowledged; the tool is
*intentionally* a sensitivity-analysis framework rather than a
definitive computation. Readers who disagree with individual
point estimates are invited to modify them in the script and
re-run. The contribution is not a specific number but a
*framework* that makes the reasoning auditable and the sensitivity
to specific assumptions explicit. This is Tucker's 2004
methodological point; the paper implements it.

## D. Specific passage objections

### D1. "Gal 1:19 is secure; mythicism is done"

**Objection.** *The biological reading of "James the brother of
the Lord" is the obvious reading. Nothing in the text suggests a
community title.*

**Response.** Engaged at length in §3.5. The "obvious" reading
is obvious only in English translation divorced from Paul's
distributional usage (117 community uses, 0 unambiguous
biological uses outside the disputed cases), from the parallel
in 1 Cor 9:5 where the plural "brothers of the Lord" is cited as
a class label alongside apostles and Cephas, and from the
well-attested Greek religious-associative convention of
*adelphoi tou* [patron figure]. Under the Pauline context and the
religious-associative convention, the community-title reading is
the parsimonious reading; the biological reading is the
interpretive stretch.

### D2. "The 500 brothers in 1 Cor 15:6 clinches it"

**Objection.** *Five hundred named witnesses to a resurrection
appearance is hard to reconcile with a purely celestial figure.*

**Response.** Engaged in §3.2 and §3.3. The "five hundred" number
is not a list of named witnesses; it is Paul's assertion that
such a group existed. The mythic-origin reading is that the
resurrection appearances of 1 Cor 15 are reports of visionary
experiences by members of the community — a phenomenon that is
attested in Jewish apocalyptic-mystical contexts (Qumran Self-
Glorification Hymn, 1 Enoch Similitudes). Multiple community
members claiming visionary experience of a heavenly figure is not
unusual in the period. The passage does not clinch the
historicist case, but the paper does acknowledge it as a genuine
difficulty.

### D3. "Josephus 20.200 is the smoking gun"

**Objection.** *"James the brother of Jesus who was called Christ"
is textually accepted by most specialists. That's independent
non-Christian attestation.*

**Response.** §6.2 addresses this carefully. The passage is
widely accepted as substantially authentic (not full-interpolation
as Carrier sometimes argues). The evidential weight is real but
limited: Josephus writes c. 93-94 CE, sixty years after the
supposed crucifixion, at a time when Christian communities and
their claims about their founder were widely circulating. His
reference attests the Christian claim as of the 90s, not
independently to a first-century individual. Both hypotheses
accommodate the datum.

## E. What the paper does NOT claim

To prevent mis-characterization:

1. **The paper does not claim Jesus definitely did not exist.**
2. **The paper does not claim mythicism has been proven.**
3. **The paper does not claim that all historicist scholars are
   incompetent or acting in bad faith.**
4. **The paper does not claim Paul fabricated or invented
   Christianity.**
5. **The paper does not deny the historical existence of Paul,
   Peter, James, or the early Christian communities.**
6. **The paper does not require the full Marcion-priority or
   late-Paul theses to be correct.**
7. **The paper does not quantify silence with false Bayesian
   precision.**
8. **The paper does not cite pagan mystery cults as primary
   sources; it cites Second Temple Jewish apocalyptic-mystical
   sources.**

## F. What the paper DOES claim

1. The evidence is insufficient to support the near-certainty the
   consensus projects.
2. The mythic-origin hypothesis, particularly in its Jewish-
   apocalyptic form, is a serious alternative that survives
   scrutiny of its own weaknesses.
3. The scholarly consensus is sustained partly by institutional
   and methodological inertia rather than by evidential weight
   alone.
4. The question should be treated as genuinely open, and the
   field's resistance to treating it as open is itself a datum
   requiring explanation.
