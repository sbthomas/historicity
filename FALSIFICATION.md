# Falsification Conditions

A companion document to *The Evidentiary Status of the Historical
Jesus*. Scientific seriousness requires being explicit about what
would falsify one's claims. This document lays out, in both
directions, what kinds of evidence would require revising the
paper's conclusions.

---

## Preamble

The paper does not assert that Jesus definitely did not exist. It
asserts that (a) the evidence is insufficient to support the
near-certainty the consensus projects and (b) the mythic-origin
hypothesis in its Jewish-apocalyptic form is a serious alternative
that survives scrutiny of its own weaknesses.

These are two distinct claims, and they can be falsified
independently. This document treats them separately.

A third-party reader will also want to know what would make the
paper **more** correct — what evidence would strengthen the
mythic-origin case beyond its current "serious alternative"
status. That is also treated below.

---

## Part I. What would falsify the paper's claim that the evidence is insufficient for historicity?

The paper claims that current evidence does not secure historicity
at near-certainty. Six categories of discovery would substantially
raise the posterior probability of historicity:

### I.1. Contemporary epigraphic attestation

**The single most decisive possible find**: an inscription or
contemporary document naming a Jesus of Nazareth from first-century
Galilee or Judea, dated to the period 20–40 CE, mentioning him in
a capacity other than as subject of Christian veneration (as an
administrative record, legal document, criminal charge,
synagogue donation, or similar).

This is the profile that settled Pilate in 1961 (the Caesarea
Maritima prefect-of-Judaea inscription, published in
\citealt{Vardaman1962}). No comparable artefact currently exists
for Jesus; one would substantially change the evidentiary
landscape. The James ossuary (2002) was a contested candidate; the
"brother of Jesus" portion of its inscription was ruled a modern
forgery by the Israel Antiquities Authority.

**Probability of such a find**: low but non-zero. First-century
Galilean archaeology continues to yield new material.

### I.2. A genuinely pre-Christian, pre-Pauline document attesting Jesus as historical

A manuscript fragment datable to before Paul's letters (i.e.,
before c. 48 CE) that clearly identifies a specific historical
figure named Jesus, independent of the Pauline claim. A Dead Sea
Scroll-style find from a Qumran or other sectarian context, dated
by palaeography and/or carbon-14 to the 30s or 40s CE, identifying
a recently-executed messianic figure by a specific name.

**Probability**: low. The Qumran material is substantially
published; a surprise comparable find is not impossible but is
unlikely.

### I.3. Independent non-Christian first-century attestation

A reference to Jesus by a first-century writer who is demonstrably
not reliant on Christian informants and who is writing
contemporaneously or near-contemporaneously. The current
non-Christian attestations (Josephus 93-94 CE, Tacitus 116 CE,
rabbinic and Mandaean much later) are all either late, dependent
on Christian sources, or both.

An example of what would qualify: a chapter of Justus of Tiberias's
lost *History* recovered from a papyrus cache, confirming that
Justus did mention Jesus (despite Photius's report to the contrary),
or a fragment of Seneca or of a lost imperial archive mentioning
Jesus in an administrative or personal context.

**Probability**: extremely low. Lost works of this kind are rarely
recovered, though not impossible (Oxyrhynchus discoveries continue).

### I.4. A demonstration that the *adelphos tou kyriou* construction is biologically specific in the Greek religious-associative register

This paper's §3.5 argument treats Greek associative inscriptional
evidence (Harland 2005; Kloppenborg-Ascough 2011) as supporting
the community-title reading of *adelphoi tou* [patron figure]. If
subsequent epigraphic research demonstrated that in Greek
religious-association inscriptions, "brother of [patron-figure]"
is consistently and reliably biological-kinship language — that
the paper's appeal to associative convention is an artefact of
cherry-picked cases — the argument would be substantially
weakened.

A systematic random-sample study of the full Greek epigraphic
corpus (PHI database; IG; SEG) for *adelphoi tou* constructions,
with rigorous classification showing that biological kinship
dominates in the construction-specific register, would undermine
the paper's principal quantitative move. This is TODO item #20 in
the research programme; it is genuinely open, and its outcome is
not predetermined.

**Probability**: unclear. The curated cases documented in the
supplement (Tanais *adopted brothers of Theos Hypsistos*, etc.)
are compatible with the community-title reading, but Harland's
own data is not a random sample and a proper sampling study has
not been done.

### I.5. Text-critical and stylometric evidence that the historicity-difficult passages are late insertions

The current analysis (§3.1, *scripts/stylometry.py*) does *not*
flag Gal 1:19 or Rom 1:3–4 as interpolation candidates. If more
rigorous stylometric methods (character n-gram analysis, POS
distributions, formal statistical testing with larger corpora
used as training/testing sets) identified these passages as
stylistically distinct in a way that mirrors the established
interpolations (1 Thess 2:14–16), the paper's text-critical
claim would need revision in the mythicist direction.

Note that this would *strengthen* the mythicist case, not the
historicist; see Part II. But the paper's current claim that
text-critical argument does not rescue the mythicist position
would be falsified in the other direction.

### I.6. Philosophical-historical demonstration that existence-question methodology differs for religious founder figures

The paper's comparative analysis (§2.5) places Jesus in the
contested-figures category alongside Pythagoras, Arthur, Homer,
and Laozi. If philosophical-historical research established that
religious-founder figures differ categorically from these cases
— that the sociological dynamics of religious-community
formation require a historical charismatic founder in a way that
other contested-figure cases do not — the comparative argument
would be weakened.

Cognitive science of religion (Boyer, Atran, Whitehouse) is
directly relevant here and has not been systematically applied to
the question (TODO item #12). A demonstration that Whitehouse's
"imagistic mode" of religion (characterised by charismatic
founder-figures and direct experience) differs from "doctrinal
mode" (characterised by transmitted doctrine and organisational
structure) in ways that make Christianity's profile incompatible
with pure doctrinal-exegetical origins would be a serious
historicist argument.

## Part II. What would falsify the paper's secondary claim that the mythic-origin hypothesis is a serious alternative?

The paper's second claim is that the mythic-origin hypothesis,
particularly in its Jewish-apocalyptic (not pagan-mystery-cult)
form, is a serious alternative. Six categories would substantially
undermine this claim:

### II.1. Systematic demonstration that Paul means biographical-Jesus by "apokalypsis"

The paper treats Paul's *apokalypsis* language (Gal 1:12, 1:16, 2:2;
2 Cor 12:2-4) as straightforward revelatory-visionary language,
fitting the Jewish mystical-ascent tradition. A demonstration
that Paul uses *apokalypsis* elsewhere in contexts where its
referent is unambiguously a recent historical event — not
visionary experience — would weaken the paper's mechanism claim.

The Greek of the passages cited is well-studied; systematic
semantic analysis of Paul's full *apokalypsis* vocabulary could
potentially yield this result, though it has not in previous
work.

### II.2. A Dead Sea Scroll or other pre-Christian document containing the full Christological sequence (pre-existence + descent + vicarious death + resurrection + exaltation)

The paper argues that Paul's Christology is assembled from
distributable Jewish apocalyptic-mystical components. A discovery
that a single pre-Christian Jewish document already contains the
full sequence would be consistent with the paper's argument — it
would not falsify it.

However, the *absence* of such a pre-Christian document is the
current state. If after another decade of Qumran and related
discoveries, no document assembles the sequence, that does not
refute the mythic-origin case (since the paper's claim is that
*components* were distributable, not that the full sequence was
pre-assembled), but it does leave the mythic-origin scenario
requiring explanation of how the components were combined.

A demonstration that the components *cannot* be combined in the
way the mythic-origin scenario requires — for reasons of
theological grammar or cognitive constraint — would be a genuine
falsification.

### II.3. Positive pre-Christian evidence of a specific historical messianic figure named Jesus

If a pre-Pauline document identifies a specific historical
messianic figure named Jesus (not a name, not a category, a
specific person), this would be incompatible with the paper's
mythic-origin scenario in its strong form. The paper does not
claim Paul invented the name — *Yeshua/Iesous* was a common name —
but the scenario does require that the community's Christ was not
a specific recent historical teacher.

### II.4. Systematic memory-theoretic demonstration that the gestalt shape of the tradition requires a historical subject

\citet{Allison2010,Allison2021} and the memory-theory school
(Keith, Le Donne, Rodríguez, Crossley) argue that the overall
shape of the Jesus tradition — certain stable motifs and
narrative elements across otherwise-independent witnesses — is
better explained by memory than by literary construction. The
paper acknowledges this is the most sophisticated historicist
argument.

A formal demonstration that the gestalt *requires* memory-style
transmission and cannot be generated by the scriptural-exegesis
+ visionary-experience mechanism that the paper proposes would
be a serious falsification. Such a demonstration has not been
made; Allison's argument is gestural rather than formal. A
rigorous cognitive-science-of-religion treatment of the
transmission-mechanism question is TODO item #13.

### II.5. A coherent demonstration that Rom 1:3–4, Gal 4:4, or 1 Cor 15:6–8 cannot be accommodated in the mythic-origin scenario

The paper acknowledges these passages as difficulties and offers
possible readings. The readings are defensible but not fully
satisfying. If specialist scholarship demonstrated that the
Carrier-style "sublunar realm" reading of *kata sarka*
systematically fails, or that 1 Cor 15:6-8's witness list cannot
be accommodated in a visionary-experience framework, the
mythic-origin case would be materially weakened.

The *kata sarka* argument is specifically vulnerable: if
Novenson's work on Davidic messianic grammar is right and
"descended from the seed of David" in Second Temple Jewish
discourse is necessarily a human-genealogical claim, the
mythic-origin reading of Rom 1:3 requires interpretive work that
the historicist reading does not.

### II.6. A demonstration that Paul's "community title" reading of *adelphos tou kyriou* is semantically implausible

The current epigraphic evidence (Harland 2005 and the 25
inscriptional cases in the baseline supplement) supports the
reading. If random-sampled epigraphic analysis of the full PHI
corpus finds that *adelphos tou* + [patron figure] is almost
always biological kinship and the Tanais/Sarapis cases are
outliers, the paper's appeal to religious-associative convention
would be weakened.

## Part III. What would strengthen the mythicist case beyond its current "serious alternative" status?

The paper's conclusion places the question in open-question
territory (Bayesian posterior 0.23–0.39 under plausible
assumptions). Three kinds of discovery would shift the posterior
substantially toward mythic-origin:

### III.1. Direct textual evidence that Gal 1:19's "brother of the Lord" clause is an interpolation

A P46-age or earlier Pauline papyrus lacking the clause, or a
patristic citation of Galatians 1 that demonstrably omits it,
would be consequential. P46 is the current earliest witness; a
pre-P46 fragment is in principle recoverable (Oxyrhynchus has
produced smaller but important Pauline fragments).

### III.2. Systematic demonstration that the Gospels predate Paul

Alternative chronology proposals (Crossley on Mark c. 45 CE;
Robinson on a pre-70 CE dating of the whole corpus) are minority
positions but have specialist defenders. If evidence emerged
that would require dating Mark earlier than the Pauline letters,
the source-trajectory argument of §9 would collapse, which would
substantially damage the paper. But the alternative dating also
damages the mythic-origin case (it would make a purely
exegetical-visionary origin of the Jesus tradition much harder
to defend). The argument here is complex.

### III.3. Direct evidence for a named "Jesus" / "Son of God" figure in pre-Pauline Jewish apocalyptic literature who matches Paul's Christ

The paper's §8 argues that components of Paul's Christology are
attested pre-Christian. A demonstration that a specific figure
named *Iesous* (Joshua / Yahweh-saves) already served as a
heavenly mediator in a Qumran-era Jewish-sectarian community would
be consequential. A proposed candidate is "Melchizedek" in
11QMelch, treated by some as a named heavenly mediator. An even
stronger parallel would be a pre-Christian text treating a
specific *Iesous* as the named pre-existent heavenly figure.

## Part IV. What the paper deliberately does not try to falsify

Some claims the paper could plausibly be read as making are
deliberately not treated as its thesis, and the paper's argument
does not require them:

1. **That Paul invented Christianity** — the paper does not claim
   this. Paul inherits pre-Pauline creeds, community structures,
   and soteriological language.

2. **That Jesus definitely did not exist** — the paper does not
   claim this and has explicit "what the paper does not claim"
   statements in the abstract, conclusion, and RESPONSES.md.

3. **That the mythicist scenario is proven** — the paper says it
   is a serious alternative that survives scrutiny, not that it
   is more probable than historicity.

4. **That the consensus is in bad faith** — the paper offers a
   Kuhnian explanation of consensus dynamics that does not
   require individual bad faith.

5. **That every New Testament figure is unhistorical** — Paul,
   Peter, James are treated as historical persons throughout.
   The question is specifically about Jesus.

These distinctions matter because reviewers are likely to
misattribute the paper's claims, and falsification attempts
against claims-not-made are not falsifications of claims-made.
