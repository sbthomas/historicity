# Classification scheme — *adelph-* family baseline study

Target lexemes: ἀδελφός (G80), ἀδελφή (G79), ψευδάδελφος (G5569), φιλαδελφία (G5360), φιλάδελφος (G5361), ἀδελφότης (G81).

Every occurrence is classified into **exactly one** of five categories. If two readings are equally defensible, assign **C**.

---

## A — Community / voluntary association

Member of a religious, philosophical, professional, or cultic group joined by election rather than birth. The relationship is constituted by shared ideology, ritual, or practice.

**Includes:**
- Christian "brothers and sisters in Christ"
- Jewish brothers/sisters in synagogue contexts where the reference is to the assembly rather than the ethnos
- Guild members, cult initiates, members of religious associations (*thiasoi*, *koina*, *synodoi*)
- Stoic/Epicurean philosophical "brothers"
- Pseudo-brothers (false brothers) — the "pseudo" prefix presupposes a community frame
- Abstract brotherhood (*philadelphia*) where the community reference dominates

**Rule of thumb:** if the "brother" is a brother *because he joined the same movement*, it is A.

## B — Biological sibling

Shares at least one biological parent with the referent. Blood relationship through descent.

**Includes:**
- Full siblings (same mother and father)
- Half-siblings explicitly
- Step-siblings only if the text frames them as "brother" in a kinship sense, not narrative adjacency

**Rule of thumb:** if replacing "brother" with "cousin" or "fellow citizen" would falsify the text, it is B.

## C — Disputed / ambiguous

Context admits both a kinship (B or D) reading and a community (A) reading, or neither reading is clearly dominant. When the text genuinely cannot be resolved, the token is C.

**Rule of thumb:** if a competent reader could defensibly classify the token as either A or B/D, and nothing in the context decisively favors one over the other, it is C.

## D — Ethnic / tribal / covenantal

Shared identity larger than family but acquired by birth (ethnicity, nation, tribe, covenantal descent). Most common in Jewish-Greek literature reflecting Hebrew *'ach* used for "fellow-Israelite."

**Includes:**
- "My brothers, my kinsmen *kata sarka*" (fellow Jews)
- Tribal kinship ("your brothers of the tribe of X")
- Descent-group solidarity larger than immediate family
- Fellow-Hellenes, fellow-Macedonians used as ethnic marker

**Distinction from A:** D relationships are inherited; A relationships are elected.

## E — Literary / metaphorical extension

Poetic, rhetorical, or philosophical figure of speech. The referent is not a person identified as a brother by community membership, biology, or ethnicity, but rather the word is used for literary effect.

**Includes:**
- Poetic pairings ("the sun, brother of the moon")
- Rhetorical solidarity ("brothers in suffering")
- Philosophical cosmic-kinship ("all humans are brothers")
- Animal "brothers" in fables

---

## Edge-case guidance

- **Vocative addresses to a mixed audience** ("brothers, I urge you"): A when the speaker identifies with them as co-members of a movement; D when the addressees are fellow-ethnic-group without a movement frame.
- **"Brother of the Lord" constructions**: always C (this is what the study is measuring).
- **Believing spouses in 1 Cor 7-style contexts**: A. The "brother/sister" marks religious community membership, not marriage.
- **Named coworkers ("Timothy the brother")**: A if the movement context is explicit; otherwise assess case by case.
- **Biographical references to someone's family members in narrative**: B if the text describes them as that person's sibling in a kinship register.

## Coding notes

- Single coder (Claude, Opus 4.7). Inter-rater agreement is not assessed in this first pass; this is acknowledged as a limitation.
- Each classification includes a one-line reason. Reasons are part of the data, not decoration.
- Borderline cases default to **C** rather than the coder's best guess, to avoid systematically inflating A or B.
